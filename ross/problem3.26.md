## Problem Statement

Two balls, each painted black or gold independently with \(P(\text{Gold}) = \frac12\).

a. Given that at least one ball is gold, find \(P(\text{both gold})\).
b. Given that a randomly selected ball falls out and is gold, find \(P(\text{both gold})\).

---

## Key Observations (DO BEFORE SOLVING)
- What type of problem is this?  
  Conditional probability with independent events; difference between "at least one" and "randomly observed one" information.
- What is random vs fixed?  
  Random: colors of both balls, which ball falls out. Fixed: \(p=1/2\).
- What is the smallest useful variable definition?  
  Let \(B_1, B_2\) be colors (1=gold, 0=black). Independent Bernoulli(1/2).

## My First Attempt

Part (a): Sample space \(\{GG, GB, BG, BB\}\), each \(1/4\).  
Given at least one gold → \(\{GG, GB, BG\}\), so \(P(both) = 1/3\).

Part (b): Naive shortcut: "Given one is gold, other is gold with prob 1/2" — I thought that works. But actually, is that always valid?

Reason: If a *random* ball is observed gold, the other ball's color is still independent? Not exactly — but in this symmetric case, \(P(\text{other gold} \mid \text{observed gold}) = P(\text{other gold}) = 1/2\) because before conditioning, colors independent, and observation doesn’t break symmetry. But let's check formally.

---

## Solution Strategy
- Step 1: Define \(A\) = at least one gold. Compute \(P(both \mid A)\) by counting.
- Step 2: Define \(E\) = random ball falls out and is gold.
- Step 3: Compute \(P(E)\) via total probability over \(GG, GB, BG, BB\).
- Step 4: Compute \(P(both \mid E)\) via Bayes.

Why this works: The falling ball provides *which* ball is gold, not just that at least one exists. This changes the conditional probability.

---

## Full Solution

**Part (a):**  
\(\Omega = \{GG, GB, BG, BB\}\), each with prob \(1/4\).  
\(A = \{GG, GB, BG\}\), \(|A| = 3\), \(|A \cap \{GG\}| = 1\).  
So \(P(both \mid A) = \frac{1/4}{3/4} = \frac13\).

**Part (b):**  
Let \(E\) = the ball that falls out is gold.  
Given \(GG\): \(P(E \mid GG) = 1\), contribution \(1/4\).  
Given \(GB\): \(P(E \mid GB) = 1/2\), contribution \(1/8\).  
Given \(BG\): \(P(E \mid BG) = 1/2\), contribution \(1/8\).  
Given \(BB\): \(P(E \mid BB) = 0\).

So \(P(E) = 1/4 + 1/8 + 1/8 = 1/2\).

\(P(both \cap E) = P(GG \cap E) = 1/4\).  
Thus \(P(both \mid E) = \frac{1/4}{1/2} = \frac12\).

---

## Final Answer

\[
\boxed{\frac13, \ \frac12}
\]

## Pattern Recognition
- Pattern type: "At least one" vs "randomly observed one" in conditional probability.
- Key trick: In symmetric independent case with random observation, \(P(other\ gold \mid observed\ gold) = P(other\ gold)\).
- Similar problems: Boy/girl paradox (Mr. Smith has two children, at least one boy vs you see a boy), Monty Hall, two-child problem variants.

---

## Key Insight (1–2 lines)

"At least one gold" treats all mixed states equally.  
"Random ball is gold" gives twice the weight to \(GG\) because it always shows gold, while mixed states only show gold half the time. That’s why probability rises from \(1/3\) to \(1/2\).

## Anki Card: When Can You Simplify the Sample Space?

**Front:**

When solving a conditional probability problem, when can you just **reduce the sample space** to the conditioning event and count equally likely outcomes?

**Back:**

You can **reduce the sample space** (i.e., just count favorable / total in the reduced set) **if and only if**:

1. **All outcomes in the original space are equally likely**, **and**
2. **The conditioning event is a subset of outcomes** (not a more complex condition that changes relative probabilities of remaining outcomes).

**When you CANNOT** just reduce the sample space:

- When the condition involves **random selection** or **observation** that makes some outcomes more likely to produce the observed evidence than others.  
  Example: "A random ball falls out and is gold" — in original space \(GG, GB, BG, BB\) equally likely, but given the evidence, \(GG\) is more likely to produce it than \(GB\) or \(BG\), so you must use Bayes.

- When the condition is **not just "is in set A"** but is **generated by a random process** (like Monty Hall opening a door).

**Rule of thumb:**  
If the conditioning event is simply "the outcome lies in subset \(A\)" of original equally likely space → reduce space.  
If the conditioning event is "a randomly selected part of the outcome has property \(X\)" → use Bayes or careful probability tree.
